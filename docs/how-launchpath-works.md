# The LaunchPath Method

How we think about picking a niche, crafting an offer, writing your business story, setting your pricing, and building a guarantee — and why.

---

## The Business Model

Everything in LaunchPath is built around one model: **build once, sell to many**.

You pick a specific type of local business — roofers, dentists, landscapers — and you solve one specific problem they all share. You build the solution once, then sell it to multiple businesses in that same niche as a monthly recurring service.

You're not a freelancer doing custom work for each client. You're not an agency juggling ten different industries. You find one painful problem that hundreds of businesses in the same niche all have, you build one solution, and you sell it over and over.

Why this model specifically? Because it's the fastest path to recurring revenue for someone starting from zero. Custom work doesn't scale. Agencies burn people out. SaaS requires months of development before you earn a penny. But a productised service — one niche, one problem, one solution, monthly fee — can get you to your first paying client in weeks, not months.

We don't offer this as one option among many. It's the only model. Giving a beginner a choice between freelancing, productised services, consulting, and SaaS would cause analysis paralysis. One model, executed properly, beats five half-built ones.

## How We Think About Picking a Niche

Most people pick a niche based on what sounds interesting or what they saw someone else succeed with. That's backwards. A niche should be chosen based on whether the economics actually work — not whether it sounds cool.

We evaluate niches on four criteria. We call this the **SERGE framework**.

### 1. ROI from Service — "Will they actually make money from this?"

The single most important question. If a business buys your service, does it directly make them money or save them measurable time?

A lead qualifier for a roofing company that books 5 extra jobs per month at £8,000 each generates £40,000 in new revenue. Charging £500/month for that is a no-brainer — it's an 80x return. That's a strong niche.

Compare that to, say, a social media scheduler for a yoga studio. It might save them 2 hours a week, but there's no direct line from "posted on Instagram" to "earned more money." The ROI is fuzzy. Harder to sell, easier to cancel.

The best niches are ones where you can point to a specific number and say: "This will make you £X more per month." If you can't draw that line clearly, the niche is weaker.

### 2. Can Afford It — "Is your fee pocket change or a real decision?"

Your monthly fee needs to be insignificant relative to the client's revenue. The rule of thumb: **your fee should be less than 2% of their monthly revenue**.

A dental practice doing £30,000/month can afford £500/month without a second thought. A window cleaner doing £3,000/month — that same £500 is a serious budget decision.

This doesn't mean you can't serve smaller businesses. It means you price accordingly. The window cleaner gets a £150/month service. The dental practice gets a £500–800/month service. The niche determines the price ceiling, not the other way around.

Higher-value niches (dental, legal, real estate) naturally support higher fees. Lower-value niches (cleaning, pest control, pressure washing) need competitive pricing but can make up for it in volume — there are thousands of them in every city.

### 3. Can Guarantee Results — "Can you promise something specific and deliver it?"

This is what separates a strong niche from a weak one. Can you make a specific, measurable promise and keep it within 30 days?

"We'll qualify your inbound leads and book 3+ appointments per week" — that's specific, measurable, and achievable. A roofer can verify it by looking at their calendar.

"We'll improve your online presence" — that's vague. How do you measure it? When does the client know it's working? They don't. So they cancel after two months because they "didn't see results."

The best niches have bottlenecks that produce **countable outputs**: leads generated, appointments booked, quotes sent, enquiries qualified. Things you can point to and say "look, here are the results."

If the result is subjective ("better brand awareness," "improved customer sentiment"), it's harder to guarantee, which makes it harder to sell and harder to retain.

### 4. Easy to Find — "Can you find 50 prospects in an afternoon?"

You've got the perfect niche, a great offer, strong pricing. But if you can't find the businesses to sell to, none of it matters.

The best niches are ones where prospects are **publicly listed and easy to contact**. Roofers, plumbers, dentists, landscapers — they're all on Google Maps, Yelp, industry directories, and local listings. You can build a prospect list of 100 businesses in an hour with nothing more than a browser.

Compare that to, say, "SaaS startups in the pre-seed stage." Where do you find them? LinkedIn maybe, but you're competing with every other B2B service for their attention. They're not publicly listed. You can't just search Google Maps for them.

The scoring scale:
- **Excellent**: Google Maps, Yelp, public directories — visible and plentiful
- **Good**: LinkedIn, niche-specific directories — findable with some effort
- **Challenging**: Requires conferences, referrals, or paid data — slow going
- **Poor**: Hard to even identify who they are — avoid

### The Bottleneck Filter

Even if a niche scores well on all four criteria, there's one more check: **is their main problem something your service can actually solve?**

The problems that work:
- They're losing leads because nobody's following up fast enough
- They're missing calls and enquiries go cold
- They're spending hours manually qualifying prospects who aren't a good fit
- They can't give quick quotes so prospects go to competitors
- They're drowning in admin for new client onboarding

The problems that DON'T work (no matter how real they are):
- They need more brand awareness (that's a marketing problem, not a service problem)
- They can't hire enough staff (that's an HR problem)
- They have supply chain issues (that's operational)
- They face regulatory compliance challenges (that's legal)

A niche can score 95/100 and still be wrong if the core problem isn't one you can solve. We'd rather recommend a 75-scoring niche where you can genuinely deliver results than a 95-scoring niche where the actual bottleneck is outside your control.

### Niches We Actively Avoid

Three niches that look appealing on paper but consistently underperform:

- **Restaurants** — Razor-thin margins (3-5%), extremely high failure rate, owners are stretched too thin to adopt new services, and they churn fast
- **Insurance agents** — Heavily regulated industry, long sales cycles (months, not weeks), compliance requirements make it hard to guarantee anything specific
- **Car dealerships** — Complex multi-step sales process, heavy existing CRM lock-in (they're already paying for DealerSocket or similar), and the decision makers are hard to reach

These aren't bad businesses. They're bad *first niches*. If someone specifically wants to serve one of them, fine — but we won't recommend them proactively.

## How We Think About Crafting Your Offer

A niche alone isn't a business. You need an offer — a clear, compelling answer to "what do I get and why should I care?" Most people skip this or make it generic. We break the offer into three parts that work together.

### Part 1: The Transformation Story

Every strong offer tells a before-and-after story. Not about your service — about your client's life.

**The "Before" (where they are now)**

This is the pain. But it has to be written in the prospect's language, not yours.

A roofer doesn't think "I have suboptimal lead conversion rates." They think "I'm losing jobs because I can't get back to people fast enough. By the time I call them back, they've already hired someone else."

A dentist doesn't think "my patient acquisition funnel is underperforming." They think "I've got empty chairs on Tuesday afternoons and my receptionist is too busy to chase up cancellations."

The before-state has to be so specific that the prospect reads it and thinks: **"That's exactly my problem."** If it could apply to any business in any industry, it's too generic and it won't resonate.

Rules for a strong "before":
- Write it from THEIR perspective, not yours
- Use THEIR vocabulary (jobs, calls, estimates — not "leads," "conversions," "funnels")
- Reference the specific bottleneck you identified in the niche analysis
- 2–3 vivid sentences, not a paragraph

**The "After" (where they'll be)**

This is the outcome. It has to be specific and measurable — not aspirational fluff.

Bad: "You'll have a thriving business with more customers."
Good: "Every enquiry gets qualified in under 60 seconds. You'll book 3+ appointments per week without hiring a receptionist or missing another call."

The after-state must contain numbers or measurable indicators. "More leads" is weak. "10+ qualified leads per month" is strong. "Better customer experience" is vague. "Every enquiry answered within 2 minutes, 24/7" is concrete.

**The System Description (what you actually deliver)**

One sentence. Describes the outcome, never the technology.

This is a critical principle: **your prospects don't care how it works. They care what it does.**

Bad: "An AI-powered natural language processing system that uses machine learning to classify and route inbound enquiries."
Good: "An automated lead qualifier that scores every enquiry and routes the hot ones to your phone in under 2 minutes."

Both describe the same thing. The first sounds like a tech pitch. The second sounds like a business solution. Your clients are roofers and dentists, not CTOs. Talk about outcomes.

### Part 2: The Guarantee

A guarantee does two things: it removes risk for the buyer, and it justifies a premium price.

"Satisfaction guaranteed" is meaningless. Everyone says it. Nobody believes it. A strong guarantee is specific to your niche and tied to a measurable outcome.

**Three types of guarantees:**

**Time-bound**: Best when your setup is fast and visible.
> "Your lead qualifier will be live and scoring real enquiries within 7 days, or you don't pay the setup fee."

This works because it's specific (7 days), measurable (live and working), and the risk is on you (no setup fee if you miss it). The client knows exactly what they're getting and when.

**Outcome-based**: Best when your service produces countable results.
> "We guarantee at least 10 qualified leads per month, or your next month is free."

This is the strongest type of guarantee because it ties directly to the value proposition. But only use it when you're confident the numbers are achievable. Promising 10 leads when 5 is realistic will destroy trust.

**Risk-reversal**: Best for sceptical niches or when you're just starting out.
> "Try it for 30 days. If you don't see the value, cancel with no questions asked and I'll refund your setup fee."

Less powerful than the first two, but much safer for you as the seller. Good for your first few clients when you're still building confidence in the numbers.

**What makes a guarantee bad:**
- It's generic ("100% satisfaction guaranteed" — says nothing)
- It promises something you can't control ("We guarantee you'll close 5 new deals" — you don't control their sales team)
- It's too generous ("Full refund if you're not happy after 6 months" — you'll go broke)

The best guarantees are specific, measurable, tied to what your service actually produces, and achievable within the first 30 days.

### Part 3: Value-Based Pricing

Most beginners price based on effort: "It took me 4 hours to set up, so I'll charge £200." This is backwards. **Price based on the value you create for the client, not the effort it takes you.**

If your service generates £40,000 in new jobs for a roofer, charging £500/month isn't expensive — it's a 6,600% ROI. The fact that it only took you 2 hours to configure is irrelevant. What matters is what it's worth to them.

**The pricing structure: Setup + Monthly**

- **Setup fee** (one-time): Covers your initial configuration work. Ranges from £200 for simple setups to £3,000 for complex ones. Also serves as a commitment filter — clients who pay setup fees are more serious.
- **Monthly fee** (recurring): This is your real income. The recurring revenue that compounds as you add clients. Ranges from £200/month to £2,500/month depending on the niche.

**How your revenue goal determines your pricing:**

This is the most practical part. Work backwards from what you need to earn:

| Your Goal | What to charge monthly | Clients needed |
|---|---|---|
| £500–1k/month | £400–500/month | ~2 clients |
| £1–3k/month | £500–800/month | ~3 clients |
| £3–5k/month | £800–1,500/month | ~3–4 clients |
| £5–10k+/month | £1,500–2,500/month | ~3–5 clients |

Notice you never need more than 5 clients to hit even the highest goal. That's the power of the "build once, sell many" model with premium pricing. You don't need 50 clients at £100/month. You need 3–5 clients at a price that reflects the value.

**What determines the fee level:**

The niche itself sets the ceiling. Higher-value niches support higher prices:
- **Dental, legal, real estate**: £800–2,500/month — these businesses have high revenue per customer and can easily justify premium services
- **HVAC, roofing, plumbing**: £500–1,000/month — good revenue, clear ROI, lots of them
- **Window cleaning, pest control, pressure washing**: £200–500/month — lower revenue per job, so keep it competitive — but there are thousands of them

**Price anchoring — how to make your fee feel reasonable:**

When you tell a roofer "£500 per month," their first thought is "that's expensive." You need to reframe it. We do this with comparable services:

- "Your Yellow Pages listing costs £400/month and brings you maybe 2 leads. This brings you 10+ qualified leads."
- "A part-time receptionist costs £1,200/month. This does the same job, 24/7, for less than half the price."
- "You spent £3,000 on a website that sits there. This actively brings you business every day for £500/month."

Always anchor against something they're already paying for — or something they clearly need. It makes your price feel like a bargain rather than an expense.

### How the Three Parts Work Together

This is what most people get wrong. They write the transformation story, then the guarantee, then the pricing — as three separate things. They don't connect.

Your offer should tell one coherent story:

1. **The transformation** describes the specific outcome ("book 3+ appointments per week")
2. **The guarantee** promises that exact outcome ("we guarantee 10+ qualified leads per month or your next month is free")
3. **The pricing** is justified by that outcome ("£500/month for a service that generates £40,000 in new business")

If your transformation says "book appointments" but your guarantee says "improve your online presence" — you've lost coherence. The prospect gets confused. Confused prospects don't buy.

Every piece of your offer should reinforce the same message: **here's the specific problem, here's the specific result, here's why the price is worth it, and here's why there's no risk**.

## How We Think About Your Demo Page

The demo page is the proof. It's where your prospects see the service in action before they buy.

### The Headline

Your headline is derived directly from your transformation story. It's not invented from scratch — it's the "after" state, compressed into 12 words or fewer.

Four formulas that work:

- **Outcome + Timeframe**: "Qualified roofing leads in under 60 seconds — no receptionist needed"
- **Before/After**: "Stop losing leads to voicemail. Start booking jobs automatically."
- **For Who + Benefit**: "For roofers who want more booked jobs, fewer wasted calls"
- **Problem Solved**: "The missed-call problem that costs you £10k/month — solved."

Pick the one that feels most natural for the niche. Home services tend to respond well to direct problem/solution language. Professional services prefer the "For who + benefit" style.

### The Qualifying Form

This is not a contact form. It's a qualifying tool. Every field has a purpose: to determine whether this prospect is a good fit and how valuable they are.

**Principles:**
- **3–5 fields only.** Every additional field kills conversion. If a field doesn't directly help you assess the lead, remove it.
- **Easy questions first.** Name and company name are low-friction. Put them first. Qualifying questions (company size, budget, timeline) come after.
- **Email OR phone, never both.** Asking for both feels invasive and drops completion rates.
- **Every field must influence the score.** If you're collecting "company name" just to be polite but it doesn't affect whether the lead is good or not — remove it or replace it with something that does.

**Example for roofing:**
1. Your name
2. Type of work needed (select: Residential re-roof / Commercial / Repairs / Storm damage)
3. Approximate roof size (select: Small / Medium / Large / Not sure)
4. How soon do you need this done? (select: Emergency / This month / Next few months / Just getting quotes)
5. Best email to send the estimate

Each of those fields tells you something: job type tells you value (commercial > residential > repairs), size tells you revenue, timeline tells you urgency, and the email gets you the lead. Four qualifying dimensions in five simple fields.

### Lead Scoring

When someone fills out the form, they get scored. Not a generic "thanks for your enquiry" — an actual assessment of how good a fit they are.

The scoring is niche-specific. For the roofing example:
- **HIGH priority**: Commercial work or full re-roof, large property, emergency or "this month" timeline → estimated value £15,000–35,000
- **MEDIUM priority**: Residential re-roof, medium property, "next few months" timeline → estimated value £8,000–15,000
- **LOW priority**: Small repairs, "just getting quotes," no urgency → estimated value £500–2,000

This does two things. First, it shows the prospect that you understand their business (they see a relevant, intelligent response — not a generic "we'll be in touch"). Second, it gives your client — the roofer — immediate value. They know which leads to call first.

## The Strategic Thinking Behind It All

### Why only one business model?

Because decision fatigue kills beginners. If we offered freelancing, productised services, consulting, and SaaS as options, most people would spend weeks researching which one is "best" and never start. One model, clearly explained, with a clear path to revenue. That's what gets people moving.

### Why honest scores?

It would be easy to make every niche score 85+ so people feel excited. But that leads to bad decisions. A niche that scores 68 is "viable but challenging" — and the person should know that before committing. Realistic scoring builds trust and leads to better outcomes.

### Why we don't let your self-diagnosis limit the analysis

When someone says "I failed because I couldn't find prospects," their diagnosis might be wrong. Maybe the real issue was that their niche was genuinely hard to find (low "Easy to Find" score), not that they were bad at outreach. Or maybe their offer was weak so even the prospects they found didn't convert.

We use the self-diagnosis for empathy and conversation tone — but the full analysis runs regardless. Better to surface the real problem than to confirm a potentially wrong assumption.

### Why no tech language in offers

Your clients are tradespeople and small business owners. They don't care about AI, algorithms, or machine learning. They care about "more booked jobs," "fewer missed calls," and "less time on the phone." Every piece of client-facing copy describes what the service DOES, not how it works. This isn't about hiding the technology — it's about speaking the client's language.

### Why 3 recommendations, not 1 or 10

Three is the sweet spot. One feels like you have no choice. Ten causes paralysis. Three gives you a genuine comparison — typically a "safe" option, a "stretch" option, and a "surprise" option — without overwhelming you. The exception: if someone's biggest problem is switching between ideas, they get one strong recommendation. More options would feed the exact problem they came to solve.

### Why the guarantee matters more than most people think

A strong, specific guarantee is the single biggest lever for charging premium prices. "Try it for 30 days risk-free" lets you charge 2–3x more than a competitor who says "no contracts, cancel anytime." Both offer the same flexibility. But the guarantee version frames your confidence in the outcome. It says "I'm so sure this works that I'll put my money where my mouth is." That confidence is contagious.
