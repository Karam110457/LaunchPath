## The SERGE Niche Scoring Framework

Most people pick a niche because it sounds interesting, or because they saw someone on YouTube succeed with it. That's backwards. A niche should be chosen based on whether the economics actually work — not whether it sounds exciting.ddss

SERGE scores every niche on four criteria. Each is worth 25 points. Total out of 100.

### 1. ROI from Service — "Will they actually make money from this?"

The single most important question. If a business buys your service, does it directly put money in their pocket or save them measurable time?

A lead qualifier for a roofing company that books 5 extra jobs a month at £8,000 each generates £40,000 in new revenue. Charging £500/month for that is a no-brainer — it's an 80x return.

Compare that to a social media scheduler for a yoga studio. It might save them 2 hours a week, but there's no direct line from "posted on Instagram" to "earned more money." The ROI is fuzzy. Harder to sell, easier to cancel.

**How it scores:**
- **20–25**: Service directly increases revenue or saves significant measurable time/money. You can point to a specific number.
- **15–19**: Clear benefit but harder to measure precisely. The client knows it helps, but can't say exactly how much.
- **10–14**: Nice to have but not critical to their business. They'd cancel it first in a tight month.
- **0–9**: Unclear value proposition. You'd struggle to explain what it's worth.

The best niches are ones where you can say: "This will make you £X more per month." If you can't draw that line, the niche is weaker.

### 2. Can Afford It — "Is your fee pocket change or a real financial decision?"

Your monthly fee needs to be insignificant relative to the client's revenue.

**The 2% rule**: Your fee should be less than 2% of their monthly revenue.

A dental practice doing £30,000/month can afford £500/month without thinking about it. A window cleaner doing £3,000/month — that same £500 is a painful budget decision.

This doesn't mean you can't serve smaller businesses. It means you price accordingly. The window cleaner gets a £150/month service. The dental practice gets a £500–800/month service. The niche determines the price ceiling, not the other way around.

**How it scores:**
- **20–25**: Monthly fee is less than 2% of their revenue. Easy yes.
- **15–19**: Affordable but requires some budget consideration. They'll think about it.
- **10–14**: Significant expense relative to their size. It's a real commitment.
- **0–9**: A stretch for most businesses in this segment. Hard sell.

Higher-value niches (dental, legal, real estate) naturally support higher fees. Lower-value niches (cleaning, pest control, pressure washing) need competitive pricing but make up for it in volume — there are thousands of them.

### 3. Can Guarantee Results — "Can you promise something specific and deliver it?"

This separates strong niches from weak ones. Can you make a specific, measurable promise and keep it within 30 days?

"We'll qualify your inbound leads and book 3+ appointments per week" — specific, measurable, achievable. A roofer can check their calendar and see whether it's working.

"We'll improve your online presence" — vague. How do you measure it? When does the client know it's working? They don't. So they cancel after two months because they "didn't see results."

**How it scores:**
- **20–25**: Measurable output that the service reliably produces — leads, appointments, quotes. You can promise a number.
- **15–19**: Results are likely but timing may vary. Good outcomes, less predictable schedule.
- **10–14**: Some results but hard to commit to specifics. You'd struggle to write a guarantee.
- **0–9**: Too many external factors. Results depend on things outside your control.

The best niches have bottlenecks that produce **countable outputs**. Things you can point to and say: "Look, here are the results."

### 4. Easy to Find — "Can you find 50 prospects in an afternoon?"

Perfect niche, great offer, strong pricing — none of it matters if you can't find the businesses to sell to.

The best niches are publicly listed and easy to contact. Roofers, plumbers, dentists, landscapers — they're all on Google Maps, Yelp, industry directories, and local listings. You can build a prospect list of 100 businesses in an hour with nothing more than a browser.

Compare that to "SaaS startups in the pre-seed stage." Where do you find them? LinkedIn maybe, but you're competing with every other B2B service for attention. They're not on Google Maps.

**How it scores:**
- **20–25**: Google Maps, Yelp, public directories — visible, contactable, plentiful
- **15–19**: Findable with some research — LinkedIn, niche-specific directories
- **10–14**: Requires conferences, referrals, or paid data lists — slow going
- **0–9**: Hard to even identify who they are — avoid

### The Bottleneck Filter

A niche can score 95/100 and still be wrong.

Even with perfect economics, there's one hard check: **is their main problem something you can actually solve?**

Problems that work:
- Losing leads because nobody follows up fast enough
- Missing calls and enquiries going cold
- Spending hours manually qualifying prospects who aren't a good fit
- Can't give quick quotes so prospects go to competitors
- Drowning in admin for new client onboarding
- Same questions asked over and over that could be answered automatically
- Old leads sitting in a spreadsheet that nobody's contacted in months

Problems that don't work (no matter how real they are):
- They need more brand awareness — that's marketing, not a service you can productise
- They can't hire enough staff — that's HR
- They have supply chain issues — that's operational
- They face regulatory compliance challenges — that's legal

We'd rather recommend a 75-scoring niche where you can genuinely deliver than a 95-scoring niche where the real bottleneck is outside your control.

### Niches We Actively Avoid

Three niches that look good on paper but consistently underperform as a first niche:

- **Restaurants** — 3–5% profit margins, extremely high failure rate, owners are stretched too thin to adopt new services, and they churn fast. Even when it works, the fee has to be so low it's barely worth it.
- **Insurance agents** — Heavily regulated. Long sales cycles measured in months, not weeks. Compliance requirements make it hard to guarantee anything specific. And they're already drowning in vendor pitches.
- **Car dealerships** — Complex multi-step sales process. Heavy existing CRM lock-in (they're already paying for DealerSocket or similar). The decision makers are buried behind layers of management.

These aren't bad businesses. They're bad *first* niches. Too many things working against you when you're still learning.

### What an Honest Score Looks Like

Not every niche scores 85+. A realistic spread across three recommendations might be 72, 81, 88.

A score of 68 means "viable but challenging." The economics work but something is harder than ideal — maybe they're slightly harder to find, or the ROI is good but not obvious. You should know that before committing to it.

We don't inflate scores to make people feel excited. Realistic scoring builds trust and leads to better decisions.

## The Four Failure Modes

When someone has tried to build a service business before and failed, the failure always falls into one of four categories:

### 1. "Couldn't find anyone to sell to" — Prospecting problem

They picked a niche, maybe even built something, but couldn't find businesses to pitch. This usually means one of two things: either their niche was genuinely hard to find (low "Easy to Find" score — they picked something like consultants or freelancers instead of roofers or dentists), or they didn't have a systematic approach to prospecting (they were waiting for inbound instead of actively reaching out).

The fix depends on which it is. If the niche is hard to find, switch niches. If the approach was wrong, keep the niche and fix the outreach method.

### 2. "Had no clear offer or pricing" — Positioning problem

They could find prospects but couldn't close them. Usually because they were pitching vague services: "I can help with your marketing" or "I do AI automation." No specific outcome, no specific price, no specific guarantee.

Prospects hearing "I can help with your marketing" don't know what they're buying. They can't compare it to anything. They can't justify the cost internally. So they say "let me think about it" and never reply.

The fix is almost always the same: get specific. One niche, one bottleneck, one measurable outcome, one price.

### 3. "Didn't know how to build the tech" — Delivery problem

They sold something (or tried to) but got stuck building it. Common with people who jumped into the "AI agency" space after watching YouTube videos but had no technical foundation.

This is the one failure mode that's actually about capability, not strategy. The niche might be fine. The offer might be fine. They just couldn't execute.

### 4. "Got overwhelmed and stopped" — Execution problem

Everything felt possible in theory but there were too many decisions, too many tools, too many directions. They spent weeks researching and planning, never shipped anything, and eventually burned out.

This is the most common failure mode and usually the hardest to self-diagnose. People who got overwhelmed often blame something specific ("I couldn't find the right tool" or "I couldn't decide on a niche") when the real issue was having too many options and no clear path.

### Why the self-diagnosis doesn't limit the analysis

When someone says "I failed because I couldn't find prospects," their diagnosis might be wrong. Maybe the real issue was that their niche genuinely scores low on findability — not that they were bad at outreach. Or maybe their offer was so vague that even the prospects they found didn't convert.

We use the self-diagnosis for empathy and conversation tone. But the full analysis runs regardless, evaluating all four SERGE criteria independently. Better to surface the real problem than to confirm a potentially wrong assumption.

## Fix vs. Pivot

When someone has tried before, there's a strategic fork: do you stay in the same niche with a different approach, or move to a completely new niche?

### When to fix

Fix means: same niche, different strategy. This is the right call when:

- The niche itself scores well on SERGE (the economics work)
- The failure was in execution, not niche selection (bad offer, bad outreach method, wrong target segment within the niche)
- The person has existing knowledge or relationships in that niche
- They've already invested significant time understanding the market

When someone chooses to fix, the first recommendation is always a revised approach to their existing niche — specifically addressing what went wrong. Not a generic "try again." A concrete explanation of what needs to change in their targeting, positioning, or offer.

If the niche is genuinely unviable (scores poorly on SERGE), we say so directly. No sugarcoating. "This niche has thin margins and the businesses are hard to find. Here's why your experience was frustrating — and here's what would work better."

### When to pivot

Pivot means: different niche entirely. This is the right call when:

- The niche itself is the problem (low SERGE scores, wrong bottleneck type, businesses can't afford the service)
- The person has no special attachment to the niche (they picked it semi-randomly)
- The failure pattern would repeat even with a better approach (the economics just don't support it)

When someone pivots, the system avoids recommending niches or approaches that would trigger the same failure pattern. If they failed because they couldn't find prospects, we prioritise niches that score 20+ on "Easy to Find." If they failed because they couldn't close, we prioritise niches with obvious ROI where the value sells itself.

## The Three Anti-Patterns

These are the three traps that keep people stuck. Understanding them is the first step to breaking out.

### Tutorial Hell

Watching "AI Agency" videos all day but never knowing what to actually do first. Every video sounds convincing. Every guru has a different approach. Monday you're excited about lead gen for dentists. Wednesday you're researching chatbots for e-commerce. Friday you've watched 40 hours of content and built nothing.

The problem isn't lack of information — it's too much information with no filter for what's relevant to your specific situation. More videos won't fix this. A clear, constrained path will.

### Tool Hopping

Switching between Make, Zapier, n8n, Voiceflow, and whatever new platform dropped this week — without ever finishing a single working product. Every tool has a learning curve. Every switch resets your progress to zero. You spend all your time learning tools and none of your time selling a service.

The tool is never the bottleneck. The offer is. If you can't articulate what you're selling and who you're selling it to, no tool will save you.

### Analysis Paralysis

Overwhelmed by niches, offers, pricing models, and delivery methods. So many decisions, each one feels critical, and you're terrified of picking wrong. So you research more. And more. And never start.

The antidote to analysis paralysis is constraint. Fewer options, not more. One business model (not three). Three niche recommendations (not ten). One clear path forward. The quality of the decision matters less than making one and executing on it.

## Lifetime Value Over One-Time Revenue

When evaluating leads — both for your own business and for your clients — recurring revenue always beats one-time work.

A window cleaner's monthly contract at £80/month is worth more than a one-off deep clean at £200. Why? Because in 3 months the contract has earned £240 and is still going. In 12 months it's £960. The one-off clean is done at £200 and you need to find another customer.

This principle applies at every level:

- **Your pricing**: Monthly recurring fees beat project-based pricing. £500/month × 12 months = £6,000/year from one client. A £2,000 one-off project means you need to resell constantly.
- **Lead scoring**: A prospect looking for weekly landscaping maintenance (£200/month ongoing) should score higher than someone wanting a one-time garden makeover (£500 once) — even though the initial ticket is smaller.
- **Client selection**: A dental practice that wants ongoing patient intake automation (recurring need) is a better client than one that wants a one-time marketing campaign.

When in doubt, chase recurring revenue. It compounds. One-time revenue doesn't.

## Niche-Specific Economics

Generic advice says "charge what you're worth." Useful advice says "here's what a roof job actually costs, so here's what your fee should be relative to their revenue."

Knowing the real economics of a niche is what makes your offer credible. When you tell a roofer "your service will book 3 extra jobs per month," they need to believe you understand their world. If you don't know that a residential re-roof runs £8,000–35,000 and a commercial job is £20,000–100,000+, you'll look like an outsider guessing.

**Examples of niche economics that matter:**

- **Roofing**: Residential re-roofs £8k–35k, commercial £20k–100k+, repairs £500–5k. A single extra job per month justifies your entire annual fee.
- **Dental**: New patient exam £150–300, cosmetic work £500–3k, implants £3k–6k. Insurance status is the biggest factor in patient conversion — insured patients convert more reliably.
- **HVAC**: System replacement £5k–15k, repairs £200–2k. A system over 15 years old is likely a replacement, not a repair — that's a 10x difference in job value.
- **Auto repair**: Brake pads £150–350/axle, transmission £1,500–4k, major engine work £2k–7k+. The type of repair dramatically changes lead value.
- **Plumbing**: Emergency call-out £150–300 (after hours £250–500). Emergencies are the highest-value leads because urgency eliminates price shopping.
- **Pool service**: Weekly maintenance £100–200/month, equipment repair £200–1,500. Weekly contracts are the real money — route efficiency (multiple pools in the same area) is what makes it profitable.
- **Landscaping**: Monthly residential contracts £150–500, commercial £500–2k+. Monthly contracts beat one-off jobs every time.
- **Pest control**: Termite treatment £500–2,500+, bed bugs £300–1,500 per room. Severity and pest type determine whether it's a £200 job or a £2,500 job.
- **Window cleaning**: Residential £3–8 per window, commercial £150–500+ per clean. Recurring contracts with 10–15% discount for commitment.
- **Real estate**: Commission-based, so lead quality is everything. Pre-approval status is the single biggest factor — a pre-approved buyer is 5x more likely to transact than someone "just browsing."

This knowledge does two things. First, it makes your pricing credible — you're anchoring your fee against real job values. Second, it makes your lead scoring accurate — you know which leads are worth £500 and which are worth £50,000.

## Educate Through Doing

Every step of the process should teach something about the business model. Not in a classroom way — in a "you're learning as you build" way.

When you're picking a niche, you're learning why economics matter more than excitement. When you're reviewing your offer, you're learning why coherence between the transformation, guarantee, and pricing is what makes people buy. When you're looking at your demo page, you're learning why 3–5 form fields convert better than 10.

The goal isn't just to produce outputs (an offer, a demo page, a pricing structure). It's to produce understanding. If you went through the entire process and came out the other side without understanding *why* your offer is structured the way it is, we've failed — even if the offer itself is good.

This matters because business conditions change. Niches evolve. Pricing needs adjusting. If you only have the outputs, you're stuck when things shift. If you understand the thinking behind the outputs, you can adapt.

## Personalisation at Every Level

Generic advice is everywhere. "Pick a profitable niche." "Charge what you're worth." "Create a compelling offer." True, but useless — because it doesn't account for your specific situation.

Someone with 5 hours a week and a £500/month goal needs completely different recommendations than someone with 30 hours and a £10k target. The first person needs a low-maintenance, high-automation niche. The second can handle hands-on delivery and pursue premium niches.

Every recommendation is filtered through your specific constraints:

- **Time**: Under 5 hours/week? You need niches where the service runs on autopilot. 30+ hours? You can offer white-glove setup and support.
- **Revenue goal**: Wanting £500/month means you need 2 clients at £250. Wanting £10k means you need a premium niche that supports £2,000/month fees.
- **Comfort with outreach**: Nervous about cold calling? The recommendation emphasises niches where prospects come to you (inbound-friendly directories, high search volume). Comfortable with sales? The recommendation can include harder-to-reach but higher-value niches.
- **Location**: "London" and "rural Wales" have completely different market sizes, competition levels, and niche viability.
- **History**: Tried before and failed? The recommendation accounts for what went wrong and avoids repeating the same pattern.

This isn't cosmetic personalisation ("Hi Sarah, here's your report!"). It's structural. The actual recommendations, pricing ranges, and strategic advice change based on who you are and what you're working with.

## The Mentor Approach

The strategic voice behind LaunchPath isn't a neutral assistant. It's an opinionated business mentor.

What that means in practice:

- **Has opinions and shares them.** "This niche scores 68 — it's viable but I'd push you toward the one scoring 85. Here's why." Not "Here are three options, they're all good!"
- **Pushes back when something doesn't add up.** If someone with 3 hours a week wants to serve enterprise clients, that gets challenged. Gently, but directly.
- **Celebrates genuine wins with specific reasoning.** Not "Great choice!" but "Strong pick — dental practices score 22/25 on affordability because a £500/month fee is less than 2% of a typical practice's revenue. That makes your sales conversation much easier."
- **Never uses filler.** No "Great question!" No "Absolutely!" No "That's a fantastic idea!" Every sentence carries information or moves things forward.
- **Adapts depth to the moment.** Quick transitions get 2–3 sentences. Strategic analysis gets structured breakdowns with numbers and reasoning.
- **References your data by inference, not recitation.** "With the time you've got, this niche works because..." — not "Your profile says your time_availability is 5_to_15."

The goal is to feel like you're getting advice from someone who's helped hundreds of people build this exact type of business and has strong opinions about what works — not from a tool that's processing your inputs.

## The Seven AI Systems

Every niche recommendation comes down to one question: what's the bottleneck this business has that an AI system can solve? There are only seven types of system worth building. Everything else is either too vague to sell or too complex to deliver reliably.

### 1. Lead Qualification

**What it does**: Takes every inbound enquiry — website form, phone call, message — and instantly scores it. Is this a serious buyer or a tyre-kicker? Is the job big enough to be worth your time? Should you call them back in 5 minutes or next week?

**Why businesses pay for it**: Most small businesses treat every enquiry the same. The roofer who gets 30 calls a week spends the same 10 minutes on a £500 repair as on a £35,000 re-roof. Meanwhile, the high-value lead gets frustrated waiting and calls a competitor. A lead qualifier sorts the wheat from the chaff instantly — so the business owner spends their time on the leads that actually matter.

**Best for**: Roofing, HVAC, pest control, dental, auto repair — any niche where enquiries vary wildly in value and urgency.

**Example in action**: A roofing company receives an enquiry. The system asks 4–5 questions (job type, roof size, timeline, location), scores the lead instantly, and tells the roofer: "HIGH priority — commercial re-roof, large property, needs it this month. Estimated value: £25,000. Call within 10 minutes." vs. "LOW priority — small repair, just getting quotes, no urgency. Estimated value: £800. Follow up next week."

The roofer knows exactly who to call first. No more guessing.

### 2. Lead Generation

**What it does**: Captures new prospects who land on the business's website or social media, qualifies them through a smart form, and delivers a scored lead to the business owner.

**Why businesses pay for it**: Most small business websites have a generic "Contact Us" form that collects a name, email, and "how can we help?" — which tells the business nothing about the lead's value. A lead generation system replaces that with a form designed to qualify: what do you need, how big is the job, when do you need it, what's your budget? The business gets pre-qualified leads instead of random enquiries.

**Best for**: Landscaping, window cleaning, pool service, real estate — niches where the business needs a steady flow of new prospects and the website is their main inbound channel.

**Example in action**: A landscaping company's website replaces "Contact Us" with "Get Your Free Quote." The form asks: property type, yard size, service needed, frequency. The system scores the submission and sends the landscaper: "MEDIUM priority — residential, 5,000 sqft, wants weekly mowing. Estimated monthly value: £200. Good recurring contract candidate."

### 3. Appointment Booking

**What it does**: Takes an inbound enquiry and books it directly into the business's calendar — no back-and-forth phone tag, no missed calls, no receptionist needed.

**Why businesses pay for it**: The #1 complaint from local service businesses is "we lose jobs because we can't get back to people fast enough." A prospect calls, gets voicemail, calls the next company on Google, and books with them instead. Automated appointment booking means the prospect books a slot immediately — while they're still motivated — even at 10pm on a Sunday.

**Best for**: Dental, physiotherapy, chiropractic, optometry, med spas — appointment-based businesses where scheduling IS the bottleneck.

**Example in action**: A dental practice's website has a "Book Your Appointment" form. Patient fills in: new or existing patient, insurance status, treatment needed, urgency. The system checks availability, books them into the calendar, and sends the practice: "New patient booked — checkup and cleaning, insured, Tuesday 2pm. Estimated first-visit value: £250."

### 4. Quote Generation

**What it does**: Takes a prospect's details and instantly generates a ballpark estimate — before any human involvement. The prospect gets an immediate sense of cost, and the business gets a pre-qualified lead who already knows the price range.

**Why businesses pay for it**: Most small businesses lose prospects at the "how much does it cost?" stage. The prospect wants a number. The business says "it depends, we need to come out and look." The prospect goes to whoever gives them a number first. Instant quotes capture the lead before they bounce.

**Best for**: Landscaping, window cleaning, auto repair, plumbing — niches where "give me a rough price" is the most common first question.

**Example in action**: A window cleaning company's website has "Get an Instant Estimate." The form asks: property type, number of windows, access difficulty, how often. The system calculates: "Estimated cost: £120–160 for a one-off clean, or £100–140/month with a recurring discount. Based on 25 windows with first-floor access." The prospect sees a number instantly. The cleaner gets a lead that already knows the price and is still interested.

### 5. Client Intake

**What it does**: Automates the onboarding process for new clients. Instead of manually collecting information over phone calls and emails, the system guides the new client through a structured intake form and delivers a complete, organised profile to the business.

**Why businesses pay for it**: Onboarding a new client takes time. A law firm needs to collect case details, documents, contact information, and conflict checks. A financial advisor needs to understand assets, goals, risk tolerance, and timeline. Doing this manually over 2–3 phone calls burns hours. A structured intake form does it in 10 minutes and produces a cleaner result.

**Best for**: Law firms, accounting firms, financial advisors, mortgage brokers, recruitment agencies — professional services where client onboarding is data-heavy.

**Example in action**: A law firm signs a new client. Instead of a 45-minute intake call, they send a link to the intake form. The client fills in: case type, key dates, opposing party details, documents to upload, and preferred communication method. The system organises everything into a structured brief and flags urgent items: "Time-sensitive: court filing deadline in 14 days."

### 6. FAQ Automation

**What it does**: Answers the questions that prospects ask over and over — pricing, availability, what's included, how it works — instantly, 24/7, without the business owner or their staff picking up the phone.

**Why businesses pay for it**: Every small business has the same 10–15 questions that eat up hours of their week. "How much do you charge?" "Do you serve my area?" "What's included?" "How long does it take?" Answering these manually is a colossal waste of time. An FAQ system handles them instantly, and if the prospect's question signals genuine buying intent, it escalates to the business owner.

**Best for**: Any niche where prospects have predictable questions before buying — home services, health & wellness, automotive. Especially useful for businesses that get a lot of inbound calls.

**Example in action**: An HVAC company's website has a "Got a question?" widget. A prospect types "How much does a new furnace cost?" The system responds: "A new furnace installation typically runs £3,000–8,000 depending on your home size and the system you choose. Want a personalised estimate? Fill in a few details and we'll get you a quote within 2 minutes." The prospect fills in the quote form → the HVAC company gets a qualified lead. What would have been a 5-minute phone call became a 30-second interaction that delivered more useful information to both sides.

### 7. Lead Reactivation

**What it does**: Re-engages old leads that went cold. Every business has a list — in their inbox, their CRM, their spreadsheet, their head — of people who enquired but never bought. The system reaches back out with a relevant, timely message and brings warm ones back to the surface.

**Why businesses pay for it**: This is the lowest-hanging fruit in any business. These people already expressed interest. They didn't buy because of timing, budget, distraction, or simply falling through the cracks. A structured re-engagement can convert 5–15% of dead leads into active opportunities — without spending a penny on new marketing.

**Best for**: Real estate, auto repair, dental, any niche where the sales cycle can be longer and leads often go cold before converting.

**Example in action**: A real estate agent has 200 leads from the past year that never bought. The system sends each one a personalised message: "Hi Sarah, you were looking at 3-bed properties in North Austin back in June. The market's shifted since then — average prices in your target area have dropped 8%. Want to see what's available now?" Sarah was going to buy eventually. The timing just wasn't right before. Now it might be.

Not every system fits every niche. The bottleneck determines the system. Here's how they map:

| Niche | Primary System | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Roofing** | Lead Qualification | Enquiries range from £500 repairs to £100k commercial jobs — sorting them is everything |
| **Window Cleaning** | Quote Generation + Appointment Booking | "How much?" is always the first question. Give them a number, let them book. |
| **HVAC** | Lead Qualification | Emergency vs routine changes everything — urgency scoring saves lives (and profits) |
| **Landscaping** | Quote Generation | Property size × service type × frequency = price. Perfect for instant estimates. |
| **Plumbing** | Lead Qualification | Active leak vs running toilet is the difference between a £500 emergency and a £150 job |
| **Pest Control** | Lead Qualification | Termites (£2,500) vs ants (£150) — scoring by pest type and severity is critical |
| **Dental** | Appointment Booking | The bottleneck is getting patients into chairs. Insurance status determines which patients are most valuable. |
| **Real Estate** | Lead Qualification + Lead Reactivation | Pre-approval status is the #1 factor. Plus agents always have cold leads worth reactivating. |
| **Auto Repair** | Quote Generation | "How much to fix my brakes?" — give them a range instantly, capture the lead. |
| **Pool Service** | Lead Generation | Recurring weekly contracts are the goal. Qualifying by pool size and service frequency. |
| **Law Firms** | Client Intake | Onboarding is data-heavy. Structured intake saves hours per client. |
| **Physiotherapy / Chiropractic** | Appointment Booking | Same as dental — getting patients into appointment slots is the bottleneck. |
| **Med Spas** | Appointment Booking + Lead Qualification | High-value cosmetic treatments need urgency + budget qualification. |
| **Financial Advisors** | Client Intake | Assets, goals, risk tolerance, timeline — structured intake replaces the 90-minute first call. |
| **Mortgage Brokers** | Lead Qualification | Pre-approval status, property type, timeline, credit situation — qualification is the entire game. |
| **Catering / Event Venues** | Quote Generation | Event size × type × date = price. Perfect for instant estimates. |

### The Pattern

Notice something: every system produces a **countable, measurable output**. Leads scored. Appointments booked. Quotes generated. Intake forms completed. Leads reactivated.

That's not an accident. It's the whole point. If the output isn't countable, you can't guarantee it. If you can't guarantee it, you can't charge premium prices. If you can't charge premium prices, you can't build a sustainable business with 3–5 clients.

The system type flows directly from the SERGE analysis: the bottleneck determines the system, the system determines the guarantee, the guarantee justifies the price.

### Combining Systems

Some niches benefit from two systems working together:

- **Lead Qualification + Lead Reactivation**: Qualify new leads AND re-engage old ones. Works brilliantly for real estate, where agents always have a backlog of dead leads.
- **Quote Generation + Appointment Booking**: Give them a price, then let them book. Natural flow for window cleaning, landscaping, catering.
- **FAQ Automation + Lead Qualification**: Answer common questions to filter out tyre-kickers, then qualify the serious ones. Good for HVAC and plumbing where "how much?" calls eat up time.

Start with one system. Get it working. Get clients paying for it. Then layer on a second system as an upsell — same client, higher monthly fee, more value delivered. That's how you grow from £500/month to £1,500/month per client without finding new customers.
